Key Dates

  • 1996 - In its ruling in the Hopwood v. Texas case, the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals rules that UT Law must use a race-neutral admissions process. Soon after, Attorney General Dan Morales declares that the ruling should apply to all public schools in the state, ending the use of affirmative action in Texas.
  • 1997 - The Texas Legislature passes House Bill 588, promising automatic admission to state schools for any Texan who graduates in the top 10 percent of his public high school class.
  • 2003 - The U.S. Supreme Court upholds the use of affirmative action in the case Grutter v. Bollinger. The decision overturns the Hopwood ruling, and UT-Austin declares the next day that it will once again begin considering race when evaluating non-top 10 percent applicants.
  • 2008 - Abigail Fisher, a white student from Sugar Land, is denied admission to UT-Austin. Soon, she will sue the university claiming that she was denied because of her race.
  • 2009 - The Texas Legislature tweaks the top 10 percent rule, allowing UT-Austin to cap the number of students it automatically admits at 75 percent.
  • 2012 - Fisher's lawsuit reaches the U.S. Supreme Court.
  • 2013 - The Supreme Court issues a limited ruling, essentially upholding affirmative action without weighing in on whether UT-Austin should be allowed to continue considering race.
  • 2015 - The U.S. Supreme Court agrees to re-hear the Fisher case. During oral arguments in December, justices debated whether the top 10 percent law achieved enough diversity that affirmative action should be outlawed at the university.
