In 1990, 52 percent of Texans age 17 to 19 were white, 13 percent were black and 32 percent were Hispanic.

But you probably couldn't tell that looking at the freshmen undergradute class at the University of Texas in Austin — because in 1990, 83.5 percent of freshmen students were white, 4 percent were black and 12.5 percent were Hispanic.

In the past 15 years, Hispanics have become the majority in the 17 to 19 year old age group at 48 percent, while the white population has decreased to 33 percent and the black population remained stable at 13 percent.

You can see a slight shift in the student body at UT that reflects this demographic change — as the proportion of Hispanic students in the 2015 freshman class increased slightly to 22 percent, while the proportion of white students has decreased 73 percent.

But the enrollment gaps across the demographic groups persist. For Hispanic students, there's a 26 percent enrollment gap — meaning, the percent of UT freshman that are Hispanic is 26 percent less than the overall number of Hispanics in that age group across the state. The enrollment gap for Black students is 7 percent.
